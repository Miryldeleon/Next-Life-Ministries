LOST HISTORY

The Bible

The Bible is the foundational text of Christianity, composed of the Old and New Testaments. The Old Testament sets the stage with Jewish law, history, and prophecy, while the New Testament recounts the life, death, and resurrection of Jesus. For centuries, it was seen as the sole guide to divine truth, shaping Western faith, morality, and identity. Yet, as modern biblical scholarship grew, the canon was seen in a new light not just as a fixed doctrinal source, but as a living document embedded in its historical context. By revisiting the Bible alongside other texts, we gain a richer view of Jesus, one not limited by rigid interpretations but expanded by religious historical text.

Nag Hammadi Library

Discovered in 1945 in Egypt, the Nag Hammadi Library is a trove of Gnostic gospels. These texts present alternative views of Jesus, often emphasizing personal spiritual knowledge over institutional religion. Unlike the synoptic gospels, they stress a mystical Christ who awakens the soul from ignorance. This discovery shook religious orthodoxy; it didn’t replace the Bible but offered a missing piece—a glimpse into early Christian diversity, expanding the possibilities of who Jesus was and how his teachings could be understood.

Dead Sea Scrolls

The Dead Sea Scrolls, found between 1947 and 1956, date back to the Second Temple period. These scrolls contain copies of almost every book of the Hebrew Bible, along with other writings of the Essenes. They demonstrated how scripture was copied, preserved, and debated in a dynamic Jewish world before Jesus. Scholars realized that these ancient manuscripts gave a broader context to the Bible, showing a fluid, interpretive tradition. This reshaped biblical history, anchoring Jesus in a world alive with spiritual expectation—where the Bible was not a static rulebook, but a growing testament within a vibrant community.

Ethiopian Bible

The Ethiopian Bible is one of the oldest and most complete Christian canons, preserved for over a millennium in Ethiopia’s ancient churches. It includes books like Enoch and Jubilees, often left out of Western Bibles. Until recently, these texts were unknown to most Christians, but now, as global access grows, they offer a fresh lens on Jesus’ world. These writings reaffirm that early Christian faith was not uniform; it was a rich, diverse, and evolving tapestry. By including these voices, we see Jesus not just through one lens, but as a figure inspiring generations across cultures.

The Urantia Book

Published in 1955 and preceded the other important ancient discoveries. The Urantia Book presents a grand cosmic narrative, reassuring humanity that it is a revelation of divine truth. It bridges religious studies in science, philosophy, and theology, describing the complete life and teachings of Jesus. The Urantia Book serves as a profound interpretive bridge, offering a cohesive narrative that synchronizes the fragments found in the Dead Sea Scrolls, the Nag Hammadi Library, and the Ethiopian Bible. While these ancient texts provide invaluable archaeological and historical snapshots of early spiritual thought and Gnostic traditions, they often remain cryptic or disconnected.

When read alongside the Urantia papers, these disparate scriptures cease to be isolated relics. Instead, they become part of a unified tapestry. The Urantia Book acts as a master key, synthesizing the mystical insights of Nag Hammadi and the ancient canon of the Ethiopian Church into a modern, comprehensive understanding of the Master’s true mission and the evolution of human faith. This was Jesus' mission to free us from the control of the religious leaders, and church institutions. The Kingdom is Within.

Jesus Taught his Apostles about in detail, about the cosmology and spiritual hierarchy that exists in and or outside of the physical world as a finely organized world and has now been assembled and presented to us, on planet earth, as The Urantia Book. The book is considered the Fifth Epochal Revelation. While it is not traditionally considered canonical, its expansive vision challenges readers to see Jesus not only as a historical figure but as a central figure in a vast, purposeful universe. For Bible believers, this is not a replacement but an invitation—to reflect on how Jesus’ life might fit into a larger cosmic story—one that unites science and faith, history and mystery.

In bringing all four texts together,The Urantia Book then gives us a key to interpret the other religious text recently brought to the attention of the world,by today's advanced AI technology. We see that the life and teachings of Jesus can be understood in new dimensions. The Bible grounds us in the core narrative; the Nag Hammadi texts expand how we imagine early faith; the Dead Sea Scrolls anchor Jesus in a vibrant Jewish world; and the Ethiopian Bible reminds us of the breadth of Christian tradition. Together, they invite both committed believers and seekers to open their minds—to see Jesus as one who, across time, has inspired countless hearts, and whose message still beckons us toward a deeper, more expansive understanding of God and life.
